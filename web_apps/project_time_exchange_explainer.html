Project Time Exchange (PTS) — Explainer for Project Controls

Project Time Exchange (PTS) treats “crash” choices as a market: each activity posts discrete, credible options (weeks saved ↦ cost/risk) and project controls buys a bundle that shrinks the current CPM critical path fastest for the money. We clear with a VCG‑style rule to set fair “prices of time,” trace out a budget↦duration frontier, and emit an auditable re‑baseline you can drop straight into CPM/EV/risk to learn real crash elasticity on your top near‑critical tasks.

Outcome: a clear spend‑vs‑weeks‑saved frontier, a VCG‑efficient allocation, and a defensible audit trail for governance.

Market metaphor (one diagram)

Crash menus (per activity)  ──►  Mechanism (VCG-style clearing)  ──►  Allocation + Payments  ──►  Re-baseline
[ (d,c) pairs, risks ]            pick x to maximize W_v               p_i = externality_i        CPM/EV/risk update

Each activity “offers” duration reductions; the mechanism buys the bundle whose externality on the critical polytope yields the biggest global time save for the spend.

Explainers for a project‑controls audience

1) What is being optimized?

We pick one option per activity from its crash menu and recompute CPM; the objective is welfare:

W_v(x) := v · (T_0 - T(x)) - C(x)

where T_0 is baseline makespan, T(x) the post‑crash makespan (longest path), C(x) total crash cost, and v your value of time (cost‑of‑delay per week). Maximizing W_v gives the VCG‑efficient allocation.

2) Why VCG‑style clearing?

VCG pays/charges agents by their externality (the difference they make to others at the efficient solution), so truth‑telling is a dominant strategy under quasilinear utilities when we select the welfare‑maximizing allocation. Practically, VCG prices reveal the shadow price of time at each activity (not a single uniform rate).

Budget versus efficiency (nuance)

A hard budget constraint (Σ C(x) ≤ B) can break exact VCG truthfulness; the classical guarantee holds when we maximize welfare without a hard cap. Controls has two clean choices:

  • Run the welfare model with v tuned from cost‑of‑delay (Lagrangian form).
  • Run a budget‑feasible knapsack/MILP—still gives a frontier, but payments are advisory (prices‑as‑signals) rather than incentive‑compatible.

Objects you already know, just recast

  • Crash menu for activity i: discrete options o ∈ O_i with (d_io, c_io, r_io).
  • Selection x_io ∈ {0,1}, Σ_o x_io = 1 per activity.
  • Schedule map T(x): CPM recompute on chosen durations; critical set = zero‑float tasks.
  • Frontier: minimal T achievable at each spend level; we often pick the “knee.”

Computation path you can audit

Start with enumeration on small menus; scale up by MILP/CP‑SAT (time‑cost trade‑off with longest‑path constraints) or by local search that only explores near‑critical neighborhoods. Every trial is:

set durations → CPM forward/backward → record (cost, makespan) → retain Pareto‑optimal points

Where the value comes from (controls‑level)

  • Transparency: an auditable re‑baseline with floats/ES/EF/LS/LF for governance.
  • Prioritization: per‑activity “price of time” steers coaching, procurement, and vendor asks.
  • Learning: repeated runs reveal elasticity—which menus consistently “clear” and which are bluff.

Risk, feasibility, and guardrails

Attach risk tags to each option (confidence intervals on duration, quality, or rework); filter menus against permit/resource gates and contractual minima before the mechanism runs. Keep a no‑deficit rule and reserve a management buffer so payments don’t suggest over‑buying time you can’t safely integrate.

Integration points (no re‑plumb)

  • CPM/EV: publish the chosen durations and floats as a new baseline; EV curves use the updated plan.
  • Risk: feed distributional crash effects into your Monte Carlo; treat VCG prices as scenario weights.
  • Change control: decisions and payments are explained by externalities; that’s board‑ready traceability.

How to set v (value of time)

Back‑solve from cost‑of‑delay: v = ΔNPV or liquidated damages ÷ weeks on the governing milestone (revenue start, LD exposure, slot loss). When in doubt, sweep v to generate a welfare curve and pick the stable region where the chosen portfolio doesn’t churn.

Pilot recipe (two sprints)

Pick ~20 near‑critical activities (low float or high path count), ask each owner for 2–3 credible menu points, then run PTS weekly for two cycles. Track: spend vs weeks saved; churn in the critical set; and whether VCG “prices” match your gut on contractor leverage—adjust menus where promises proved illusory.

Externality on the critical polytope

The current schedule is the maximum over many path sums; your crash on task i only helps if it perturbs that max. VCG internalizes this: it rewards options that actually move the max, not just shorten a non‑binding path.

Failure modes (and how to pre‑empt them)

  • Non‑credible menus → require evidence (method, crew plan, historical yield) and cap per‑period crash.
  • Budget‑only governance → if you must hard‑cap, treat prices as diagnostic rather than incentive‑compatible.
  • Hidden couplings (resources, calendars) → extend to RCPSP or disallow conflicting options jointly.

Minimal math you can paste into a note

Choose x ∈ {0,1}^{|O_1|+⋯+|O_n|},  Σ_{o∈O_i} x_io = 1
C(x) = Σ_{i,o} c_io x_io,  T(x) = CPM_LongestPath({d_io x_io})
Welfare:  max_x  v · (T_0 - T(x)) - C(x)    (VCG‑truthful)

Payments in one sentence

Each activity’s charge/credit p_i equals the harm (or benefit) its presence causes to everyone else’s welfare—i.e., the gap between the best welfare when i is forced to baseline and the others’ welfare at the chosen allocation.

What you keep from “classic crashing”

All of it—PTS is a wrapper: same CPM graph, same time‑cost menus, just a mechanism that picks consistently and explains why (prices, externalities, knee).
